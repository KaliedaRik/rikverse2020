Economic Migration is an Eighty Mile Trek

My walls are packed with the produce of lands
that spatter the globe, high stacks of goods
freshly jetted from fields to joust
in 'recycle me' wraps for my wallet's change.

I buy tomatoes
from Mexico, break them
free from their prisons,
pinch and fondle
their standard sized,
unsullied flesh.
In the street, I bite,
assert my right
to consume the fruits
of alien worlds.

It's easy to amble aimless across
my gridded Eden, its angles of growth
a novel of charms, a nascent creation –
a smooth of steel, a stretch of bricks
stacked over levels of layered soils.

Beyond the walls, a wilderness
of ditches dug to drain the muds,
their looping routes layered in arches
of brambles, nettles, a bloom of hawthorn.

I turn the page, pick at the news.

Someone penned a protest note to a national rag to rage at the plans to erect an arc of energy mills behind the walls of the wilderness. "It spoils the views", they vent. "It threatens

the natural world". I wipe some pips from my chin as I read. "It wrecks the charm of this special place and the price of my house".
